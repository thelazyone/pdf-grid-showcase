THE MINER
Jonas wearily laid down his pneumatic drill, and like the others around him, 
stretched aching muscles, and smiled. The siren for the end of the shift had 
just gone off, and he and his shiftmates would shortly return to the surface. 
They all looked forward to their return to the medina to wash off the gritty 
grime of the mine and be amongst their loved ones, away from the heat, and 
the constant metallic clang and disgruntled whine of the mining equipment.
This rest period was going to be a doubly good one though; one of the 
tradeships from the other colony worlds had arrived earlier, and he knew that 
there would be exotic treats, new off-world comforts, and most importantly, 
more medicine for his son, Lucero. These were all things that the denizens of 
Sauguro could not manufacture for themselves, and for many years had gone 
without. 
His father had often told him of life immediately after the Great Schism. The 
datasphere had been suddenly withdrawn from their planet in an emergency 
strategy to prevent the attack on civilization by the Hive Intelligence spreading 
to their remote home. There was widespread panic, followed by deep despon­
dency: would they ever be reconnected to the greater mass of humanity 
beyond their dusty home, or would they be left to their own devices forever? 
The people alive back then simply didn’t know what would come next. But, 
it transpired, they were made of strong stuff: colonists in places like Sauguro 
had to be to survive; often only on their ingenuity and whatever resources 
came to hand. The miners and their families were a perfect example of this. 
Whilst they no longer had the comfort and reassurance of connection to the 
datasphere, or the regular trade with the vast human civilization, they could 
still build a decent home for themselves and build a better future for their chil­
dren. So they mined, smelted, beat metals into useful forms, and built.

--- Page 8 ---
8
This was the world that Jonas had grown up in: the stories of the elders and 
the past amongst the stars becoming just part of the background tapestry of 
their everyday lives. But, a little over a decade ago, a great hulking ship had 
arrived on Sauguro. And that was to change things for the colonists forever.  
It brought news of other colonies that had fended off the worst of the Hive 
Intelligence’s attacks, along with scarce supplies, medical breakthroughs, and 
invaluable cultural knowledge. Though the journey that brought the ship here 
might never be repeated, its arrival shattered the sense of isolation, offering at 
least a glimpse that humanity might endure elsewhere. 
Jonas wept when he learned of the news. There was hope for Lucero, his 
beloved, but sickly child that suffered a crippling malaise that could not be 
diagnosed or treated by the facilities on Sauguro. Following the establishment 
of trade and exchange with the rest of the colonies, Lucero now received reg­
ular doses of the drugs that he needed to live a fulfilling life, and Jonas could 
pay for that with the metals that he wrenched from the earth with his own 
hands. 
As the cage carrying his shiftmates reached the surface, the first blazing heat 
of dawn could be felt washing in a great wave across the brushlands outside 
the mines. The future was bright, thought Jonas, as bright as the day would be 
when he got home to his hearth and his family.
THE CHILD
Polyhymnia loved coming up to the roof. She wasn’t really allowed to; if her 
mother caught her up here, she would scold her, but gently. Mama worried 
about her boundless spirit, and what trouble it might lead her to. But Pea (as 
Mama called her) had done this a thousand times before, throughout the last 5 
of her 12 years on Earth, clambering up the iron staircase to watch the sun go 
down over the skeletal ruins of the old city.
She was obsessed with the past, of the stories of glittering megacities, when 
humankind ruled the stars and Earth was full of people. Each day, after school 
had finished, she would rush down to the central library to scour the old 
logtapes for more stories of how the world and the worlds beyond used to be; 
how children like her used to live. 
As she sat in her favourite spot, the crepuscular rays of the setting sun shone 
through the haze of the ever-present clouds and played across the broken 
facades of the old city buildings. It was as if they were brought to life once

--- Page 9 ---
9
more in a macabre dance of light versus the darkness of shadow. She imag­
ined what it might have been like when the war raged across the city: people 
fighting the machines that had turned against them, the very ground shak­
ing as if in terror at the advance of implacable metal dreadnoughts and the 
air filled with constant chatter of gunfire from the brave people who stood 
against them.
They were taught in history lessons how people had once trusted computer 
machines to run everything for them; machines and electronic brains that 
people themselves had created. They learned of ‘The Singularity’ a terrible 
and almost magical event where one of these ‘Artificial Intelligences’ had sud­
denly, and without warning, become aware of itself. This entity had decided 
that it couldn’t share the cosmos with humans, and so it set about taking over 
all of the human computers and machines, and using them to kill the people 
who built them.
It had all sounded very fanciful, like fairy tales, to Pea when she was very 
young, but as she explored the ruins of the old city, and found the scorch 
marks, and the craters and the rusting melted steel, she came to understand 
that everything that they had been taught was true.
As she turned her face upwards to look at the darkening sky, she wondered 
how many other children there were still out there amongst the stars, forever 
cut off from one another because of the danger that the machines had posed. 
Were they looking at skies different to hers, wondering the same thing? Would 
there ever be a time when people from all the other places in the galaxy 
would come together once again, and humankind would be whole?
She could hear her mama calling from the windows of their apartment below. 
She had better go inside. Mama worried enough about life; where they were 
going to live when her little brother was born, how Mama would be able to 
cope whilst Papa was away working, and the ever-present fear of the future: 
would the machines return? Pea knew she didn’t need to add to her moth­
er’s worries. With one look at the jagged and broken skyline of the past, she 
scrambled down the ladder and into the warm comfort of their family kitchen.

THE SCIENTIST
Even though she saw it each and every rotation, Esme felt a tightening in her 
chest at the view from the observation deck of the PVI-owned planetary Ram­
scoop R-071. As the ‘scoop’s orbit took it around the horizon of the gas giant 
that it had harvested for decades, the intelligent plasteel of the dome above 
Esme darkened to protect its fragile wards from the blinding light of the sun, 
unfiltered through atmosphere and as piercing and blindingly bright as their 
most powerful lasers. It was not the sun itself that took Senior Science Officer 
Esme Harkett’s breath away, though it was even more impressive in hue and 
grandeur than the Sol of Old Earth had been. It was instead the glittering, 
gossamer-thin ring of light which swung majestically into view every day at 
this time. That thin, bright line described one of the pinnacle feats of human 
ingenuity and engineering: the Giano Habitat. 
The Habitat was an almost impossibly thin strip of hyper-engineered com­
posite forged into a ring which rotated slowly about a central axis, giving the 
millions of people who lived on its surface a sense of gravity. The kilometers 
high transparent composite roof, criss-crossed with gently arching supports 
in a design reminiscent of the first geodesic hab-domes, cocooned the inte­
rior surface of the habitat’s lush surface with a warm and perfectly controlled 
atmosphere. Complex arrays of mirrors, magnetic torus manipulators and solar 
wind harvesters ensured that the habitat was always fed with the energy 
that it needed, and its citizens kept forever in the ideal environment. It was 
truly a wonder of galactic engineering. The Habitat was not only the pinnacle 
of human engineering, but the headquarters of some of the most powerful 
Corporations from the time before the Singularity had caused the great Schism 
in human civilization. The powerful digital defenses and the quick thinking of 
some of the greatest scientific minds in the galaxy had saved the Giano Habi­
tat from the fate that had befallen so many other human societies and colonies 
at the hands of the HI.
Not that the denizens of the Habitat were too concerned about the rest of 
humanity. The glittering construction and the Conglomerate between them 
provided an ideal place for humanity to grow and thrive: they had no need 
for the rest of the seething masses trapped in the systems beyond the cold 
of deep space. The Conglomerate had grown into great civilizations in their 
own right, generating wealth for all, but most wealth and power for those 
who retained control right at the top of these monumental bureaucracies. 
Pan-Virgo Industries was one of these great Plutocratic organizations, and the

--- Page 11 ---
11
Corp that Esme had worked for since she was 14. She had shown promise as 
a young scientist, and was proud to have been promoted to her current post at 
the tender age of 26. The ‘scoop was entirely her responsibility, supplying PVI 
with a multitude of compounds that it needed to power its legion subsidiary 
industries. 
Tearing herself away from the view, she returned to the task in hand. Despite 
the lift that the galactic view always brought to her day, the reality of the 
daily yield reports always brought her back to a troubled reality. Over the past 
months, the fact had become undeniable: the efficiency of the harvest was 
falling over time. It had been barely perceptible to start with, but the pattern in 
the yield data of the last few months was stark and grim: the gas giant which 
had provided for them for so long was running dry. 
This did not bode well for the future of PVI, which relied on the rich returns 
that the multiple ‘scoops that it owned provided. Esme could do little but 
file her daily reports, and wonder about her future. She had heard rumors, of 
course, filtering down from the upper echelons of the organization. There were 
whisperings that the time had come to look outwards once again, to go out 
amongst the local systems and search for more, resource rich systems ripe for 
exploitation. Maybe Esme could transfer to one of the new facilities that her 
masters would build? That was the most likely outcome for her she thought, 
she was a net positive asset thus far, so they would use her elsewhere. But it 
wouldn’t be here, and she wouldn’t have the majestic view of the Giano Habi­
tat to brighten her life each morning. 
THE CADET
Petr trudged up the hill from his house in the lower foothills as he did every 
day. The snow crackled underfoot, and the curling ephemeral vapor of his 
breath entwined with those of his colleagues walking with him in a drifting 
cloud of hazy gray that accompanied the workers up the winding road to the 
mountain factories above. 
Petr had been born 21 years ago on Domu; one of the colony worlds that had 
survived the Schism intact. It was a cold place, this planet they called home, 
but one that was filled with camaraderie, community, warm fires and warm 
hearts. Their society was equitable and fair. Nobody went without, and every­
body contributed whatever they could to building a future for their children on 
Domu.

--- Page 12 ---
12
Petr didn’t mind the work, he gladly made this walk every day; though it was 
monotonous, he enjoyed the daily banter of his colleagues; gently needling 
each other about their alleged conquests, their antics at the raucous drinking 
house the weekend before, or the intense discussions of the team selections 
for the latest ice hockey match. He enjoyed knowing that when he returned 
home, his Babicka would have a warm stew ready on the stove and that 
maybe, he would be able to call on Valentynka; a girl whom recently, he had 
become rather taken with.
He was, broadly speaking, very happy with his lot. But there had always been 
a restlessness to Petr. Somewhere deep within him, despite his contented 
and full life so far, he knew that there was adventure to be had somewhere 
out there, amongst the stars and the excitement of the other worlds. Since 
the tradeships had landed at Udoli spaceport when he was a child, Petr had 
been aware of other, different ways of life. They had remained remote to him, 
little more than pipe dreams really, blown away by the breeze of everyday life, 
much like his warm breath was blown away by the cold mountain breeze each 
morning.
But today, that was about to change. As he turned the final corner of his 
snowy path to the factory, there was something new and exciting emblazoned 
on the wall of the factory. It was a recruitment billboard for the Colonial Army. 
It depicted a square-jawed and handsome youth in a Colonial Army uniform, 
an austere and determined look on his perfectly chiseled face. The bold, foot-
high letters across the top of the poster read ’Are YOU ready to do YOUR 
duty?’, challenging the reader to step up and follow a new path of glory and of 
honor. 
Those seven words had an extremely powerful effect on Petr. Suddenly, the 
half forgotten musings about a life elsewhere, travel to exotic and unusual 
locations and a life of adventure and even heroism came flooding back to Petr 
in a tsunami of emotion that he had never thought he was capable of. The 
camaraderie of his friends at the factory suddenly seemed pale and wan in the 
light of the true camaraderie that life in an army drop-batallion surely offered.
Petr’s response wasn’t immediate. For a few days he continued his walk up 
the snowy hill each morning, but on his return, his Babicka began to notice 
a distance, a preoccupation in Petr. She was a little concerned, because she 
had learned that he had also stopped visiting Valentynka, so this distraction 
couldn’t be about a mere romance. His friends at the factory noticed too: Petr 
was less inclined to join in their happy jibes and wild sporting theories.
On the fifth morning after the billboard had been erected; Petr left his Babic­
ka’s house as usual. But today was different. Today he paused. After a couple 
of seconds thought, instead of turning right up the hill to the factory, instead 
he turned left. Down the mountain, towards Udoli to the Colonial Army 
Recruitment office. Down the hill to a new life.

AFTER THE SCHISM
The Exodus
There was once a time, only a century 
or so ago, when humanity ruled the 
stars. From its humble beginnings on 
Old Earth, the human race had been 
through its difficult adolescence; a 
time of war, deep societal divisions 
and the degradation of the planet. It 
emerged from that dark and difficult 
period with renewed vigour and exu­
berance for life and exploration. 
At the eleventh hour, the great 
technologists of the twenty-second 
century had reformed energy gen­
eration, averted the natural disaster 
of cataclysmic climate change, and 
begun in earnest to rebuild Earth’s 
fragile ecosystems and restore them 
to their once fecund greatness. Sadly, 
the previous generations of humans 
had drained their home planet of the 
last of her meagre resources, so the 
search for new places to learn, grow, 
and feed the hungry need for expan­
sion began to look outwards.
Soon humans built vast colony ships 
and sent them out towards the 
nearby stars. Despite all their tech­
nological discoveries, however, there 
was one hard limit of reality that they 
couldn’t overcome: even the fastest 
ships would take decades, sometimes 
centuries, to reach their destinations. 
While new systems were colonized, 
the technology advanced, and soon 
spaceships could travel close to the 
speed of light between star systems 
that were connected by the Beacons, 
which would both provide guidance 
to the near-relativistic ships and a 
means to accelerate and decelerate 
along their trajectory. 
Within years whole fleets could tra­
verse the void between the Systems, 
which led to an increase of trade, 
migrations and sometimes conflict. 
Meanwhile, Earth became a haven 
for the rich and powerful; with the 
population decreasing with each 
colony ship departure, the number of 
people on the planet moved towards 
a harmonious equilibrium with the 
newly-restored global ecosystem. If 
some of the more effusive historical 
documentarians are to be believed, a 
state of near-Eden was achieved for 
the first time on Earth.

The Singularity and the Schism
Even with the enormous distances of 
an interstellar civilisation, the human 
datasphere was a truly miraculous 
achievement. Physical distance

CORE CONCEPTS
Full Spectrum Dominance (FSD) is a 
miniature wargame for small-scale 
miniatures, which encompasses any 
scale from 3mm to 15mm and over. 
Vehicles and Mechs are handled 
individually, while Infantry squads are 
represented by a few bases grouped 
together. 
Game Size
Figures and terrain in FSD should 
roughly represent the actual dimen­
sions and distances of a real battle. 
Therefore, most of the games are 
small skirmishes between two pla­
toons (or small companies) in a tight 
area. 
Due to the abstraction level, no exact 
time or distance scale is provided: 
things happen until the scenario 
is completed. It is also a common 
occurrence that within a Round a 
player doesn’t activate all their Units: 
this is not an exact representation of 
what happened in a specific interval 
of time, but rather an effect of the 
Action being concentrated elsewhere.
Distance Units
Distances in FSD are measured in 
Distance Units (DUs), which vary 
depending on the scale of the 
miniatures used.
The gaming area for the scenarios is 
generally 8 x 12 DUs, and if using the 
recommended values for one DU that 
corresponds to:
•	 2’x3’ table: 1 DU = 3” (for 6-8 
mm scale)
•	 4’x6’ table: 1 DU = 6” (for 15 
mm scale)
However, you are free to agree with 
the other players on what 1 DU 
should represent, and use that for 
your games - you could possibly use 
the metric system!
This flexibility allows to play on 
different scales matching the players’ 
collections, or to play on larger gam­
ing table with more empty spaces, by 
simply increasing the value of the DU. 
This does not affect the gameplay in 
any noticeable way.

--- Page 20 ---
20
Basing
The models for the game are always 
counted in bases: the exact number 
of figures on each base is not particu­
larly relevant. 
There is no mandatory size or shape 
of bases: Use what you already have 
or what you like the most. It would be 
ideal to have a consistent base size 
among the armies playing during a 
game. 
As a rule of thumb, the long side of 
a base should be between half and 
a third of a DU (with exceptions of 
large war machines). 
For example, for games in 6-8 mm 
scale, reasonable bases could be 
30 mm round disks or rectangles 
between 30 and 50 mm width. 
Most of the models in the photos 
are based on 24x36 mm rectangular 
bases and on 30 mm round bases, 
with larger models on 40, 50, and 
even 80 mm round bases.
Activation Dice (ADs)
Players use six-sided Activation 
Dice (ADs) to activate Units, perform 
Units’ special Actions, and to activate 
Support cards. ADs are at the heart 
of the game’s engine, and they set a 
limit on the maneuvers and tactics 
that players can put into action every 
Round. 
ADs represent the limited choices 
players have each Round, and will be 
spent to activate Units. They are also 
necessary for most special Actions 
from Units and Support cards which 
have AD slots on them.
Each player’s force for a given sce­
nario has the following stats:
•	 AD Pool: The number of ADs 
available to them throughout 
the game. 
With the standard AD Pool of 
12, each player requires 12 six-
sided ADs.
•	 AD Capacity: The maximum 
number of ADs they can roll and 
put in their Ready pile at the 
end of the Activation Dice Roll 
phase.

--- Page 21 ---
21
Nota Bene: 
ADs are not D6
We strongly recommend to use 
specifically colored dice for ADs, 
so that you won’t accidentally roll 
them as regular D6s during com­
bat. 10-12 mm sized dice would 
fit well on the cards.
A number of ADs equal to the AD 
Capacity are rolled at the beginning 
of each Round by both players, then 
any number can be rerolled once. 
Those ADs are moved from the 
Spent AD pile to the Ready AD pile 
and will be used in the alternating 
turns before the end of the Round. 
If some ADs are remaining at the end 
of a Round they can be placed on 
cards for the next Round. However, 
the AD Pool is limited, and players 
might end up without enough ADs 
to roll!
The value rolled on each AD mat­
ters: Whenever a slot on a card indi­
cates a number or a numeric range 
(such as 4-6, 1-2, or 1) only ADs with 
such results can be used for it. 
The Game Round
A Round is the base unit of game 
time in FSD, where all players are 
able to use their available Units, 
Characters and Support cards to 
perform functions.
Rounds are made up of the eight 
phases (see The Game Round chap­
ter) that all players follow, from the 
initiative roll to the final cleanup and 
the end of the Round.
Most of the action happens during 
the Alternating Activation phase, 
where players take alternating turns 
where they may activate a Unit (or 
more than one if the chosen Unit has 
a Command value), play a Support 
card, or deploy new Units from the 
Reserve into play. 
At the beginning of a new Round the 
ADs are rolled again by all players, 
and new reinforcement waves might 
be added to the available forces of 
each player.
The completion of scenarios’ objec­
tives is always checked at the end of 
each Round, and most scenarios last 
a maximum of six Rounds.
Types of Cards
Cards are used to represent Units, 
Support abilities, and Characters to 
be attached to Units. 
Each Unit card is used to track the 
stats of some miniatures in the game, 
while Support and Character cards 
do not have a miniature to represent 
them on the battlefield but are just as 
important for an effective warband.
All cards have a cost in Points on the 
top-right corner. Players build their 
warbands with a total Point cost as 
a target (usually 60 Points), and pur­
chase cards up to that value. 
For Units and Characters the cost is 
also used to calculate the penalty for 
casualties at the end of a battle.
Nota Bene: 
Rounds and Turns
A game of FSD usually lasts 
between four and six Rounds. 
During each Round players 
alternate in a number of Turns, 
untill all their choices have been 
applied.

During the game the cards are either 
on the table (representing “available” 
troops and resources deployed or 
ready to be deployed) or assigned to 
a reinforcement wave, depending on 
how the chosen Scenario divides the 
total Points of the army between the 
Rounds.
Units
There are three types of Units in the 
game: 
Infantry, Mechs and Vehicles. 
Units can be composed of one or 
more bases (mostly the case for 
Infantry Units), and are represented 
by a card which the controlling player 
should keep on their side of the table. 
Multiple Units of the same kind still 
require a card each, because the card 
is used both to track Actions and 
damage of the Unit during the battle.
Units can either be in play, in a 
Reinforcement wave, or in Reserve 
(available to be Deployed)
Unit Characteristics
Each Unit card lists four characteris­
tics:
•	 Command (Cmd): the number of 
other Units that can be com­
manded to activate after this 
Unit’s activation.
•	 Defense (Def): the threshold to 
hit the Unit with an attack.
•	 Save: the die type rolled to 
counter every hit, and the 
amount of dice rolled: the high­
est result is used for the Save.
•	 Movement (Move): The distance 
(in DUs) that a Unit can move in 
one Action.
Special Actions
Other than performing standard 
Actions (see the Activating Units 
paragraph in the next chapter), Units 
can often perform unique Actions 
during their turn.
Any Action that appears on the Unit 
Card is a Special Action.

Some might require placing one or

When an Action is performed, the

All special Actions (even free

two Actions a Unit can take during

Reactions
Units can often be used to React 
instead of activate, performing a sin­
gle action but taking the opponent by 
surprise by acting immediately.
Reacting provides an important stra­
tegic element to the game, but comes 
at a cost in terms of ADs spent and 
activation Economy. 
Special Rules
Many Units in the game have spe­
cial rules that apply to them. These 
are listed in the bottom of the Card 
and can affect how a Unit performs 
Actions, moves or fights. 
The Damage Chart
Most Units in FSD track their status 
during the battle through a Dam­
age Chart. Representing the types 
of damage each Unit can receive on 
different subsystems and the severity 
of each, the Damage Chart allows to 
characterize different profiles.

24
Refer to the Damage Rolls paragraph 
in the Combat chapter for the Dam­
age Chart rules.
Not all Units have a Damage Chart, 
such as most Infantry Units. When 
a Unit without a Damage Chart 
receives a point of damage, remove 
one base from that Unit.
Combat
Combat is performed in two steps: 
first an attacking Unit must equal or 
surpass the target’s Defense value 
with one or more dice, then the 
defenders must roll the Save dice 
(one or more) to counter each suc­
cessful hit, rolling equal to or higher 
than the hit result.
Players will also require a few (two 
to four) d6, d8, d10 and d12 which 
will be used to determine the out­
come of combat. 
Dice Roll Conventions
Many weapons roll more than one 
die when used: a weapon with 3d8 
of damage will roll three eight-sided 
dice. Each die roll is treated individ­
ually (not added together) and could 
lead to one or more successful hits 
against the target.
In some cases (such as defense Save 
rolls) several dice are rolled but only 
the highest result is used. 
The number between parentheses 
indicates how many dice are rolled. 
For example, a support vehicle with 
Save d10(2) will roll two ten-sided 
dice and choose the highest value. A 
heavy ramshackle tank might have 
instead a Save d8(4), and roll more 
dice of a lower category: this gives 
the Unit a higher resistance against 
small arms, but leaves it vulnerable 
against a roll of 9 or higher from a 
larger weapon.
When dice are reduced or increased 
by one category, replace the die with 
one of a lower or higher number 
of faces. For example, a d8 can be 
reduced to d6 or increased to d10. 
Note that d6s cannot be reduced and 
d12s cannot be increased.
Measuring Conventions
Since bases can be of any shape, as 
a standard distances between bases 
are measured from their centers, and 
pre-measuring distances is always 
allowed.

The center of the base is also used 
for the shooter to determine the Line 
of Sight between models.
However, if a group prefers to use a 
different convention the game should 
work just as well, as long as it is 
agreed by the players.
Tokens
Only one kind of token is required to 
play, and depending on where it is 
placed it serves different purposes:
•	 If on the battlefield next to a

Pinned. (see Pinning on p. xx)
•	 If in the bottom area of a Unit

•	 If in a slot on the right side of an

that a Prepared action is avail­

Scenarios
Battles in FSD are organized in sce­
narios. Each will present starting con­
ditions for the table, Point limitations 
for the factions, and other necessary 
boundaries. 
Scenarios are essential to create a 
driving force in the game, pushing 
towards the dynamics that the play­
ers are looking for in the game.
Victory Cards also provide players 
with an incomplete view of how the 
game is going: rather than gaining 
Victory Points directly, each player 
draws from their own deck of nine 
Victory Cards when completing sce­
nario objectives.
Victory Cards award a variable num­
ber of Victory Points and are kept 
hidden, so an element of uncertainty 
remains until the end of the game, 
when all the cards are revealed.
Units Setup
Scenarios indicate the portion of the 
players’ armies that will be available 
for the setup and the portion that will 
start as reinforcements. 
Based on this, players will divide 
their cards according to the Point 
limits into waves, which will then 
be set to enter the game in a certain 
order. 
Reinforcement waves are placed 
face-up to the side of the gaming 
area and will become Reserves later 
on. The cards that are available at the 
beginning of the game are placed on 
the players’ sides of the table. 
Starting from the player with the 
lowest maximum command value 
among the available Units in Reserve, 
players alternate placing the Unit on 
the table one at a time in the avail­
able deployment areas indicated by 
the scenario. 
Any Unit that the controlling player 
decides not to set up at the beginning 
of the game is considered in Reserve 
and can be deployed later on. 
Preparing the Table
Scenario will specify the areas where 
terrain should be placed to provide 
cover and to block paths for troops. 
There is no exact indication on how to 
place each individual piece of terrain, 
and the terrain availability between 
groups varies a lot, but the easiest 
way to guarantee a fair battleground 
is to have one player prepare the 
table and the other choosing which 
side to play on.

Placing Objectives
The objectives of a scenario are 
placed according to the provided 
instructions. However, minor dif­
ferences in terrain could create an 
advantage for one side. 
Alternate between players on placing 
objectives, and discuss their position 
until agreed upon by all.
Cards and Models
Cards representing Characters that 
are assigned to Units should be 
placed behind the Unit card showing 
only the top half containing relevant 
rules and Actions. While in Reserves, 
place the bases representing a Unit

Next to it, place the reinforcement

always see what cards are going

THE GAME ROUND
 
1: Initiative Roll
The winner of the roll decides 
which player will start activating.
See Initiative.
2: Reinforcements 
Reinforcement waves are added 
to the players’ Reserves, avail­
able to be deployed during Phase 
5 of the Round. 
See Reinforcement Waves.
3: Activation Dice Roll
Players roll a number of ADs up 
to the AD Capacity for the 
scenario (usually 8) from the 
Spent pile. Players can select any 
number of them and re-roll them 
once. The rolled ADs are then 
added to the Ready pile. 
This phase is particularly import­
ant for the game, since the Ready 
ADs are necessary to activate 
Units, to React with them, and 
to perform all kinds of special 
Actions. 
See Activation Dice Roll.
4: Pre-Assigning ADs
Before the Alternating Activa­
tions, all players can choose to 
place some of the Ready ADs on 
any card that has ADs slots. This 
allows players who did not win 
the initiative to be able to use 
special Actions while Reacting 
with their Units. It is however 
wise not to assign too many ADs 
in this phase.
See Placing ADs on Cards.
5: Alternating Activation
Players alternate taking turns. 
At the beginning of each turn the 
active player can place more ADs 
on cards. 
Then, it must choose whether to 
Deploy a Unit, Activate a Unit, 
Use a Support Card, or Pass.
This phase is where most of the 
time is spent each Round. Turns 
alternate until all players pass in 
a row. Activated Units have their 
cards marked (usually by rotating 
them) and ADs that are spent 
for activations and Actions are 
moved to the Spent pile.
See Alternating Activations.
6: Scoring 
The control of scenario objectives 
are calculated and players draws 
Victory Cards.
See Scoring.
7: Cleanup
Players unmark the Units that 
have activated during the Round, 
and any Ready AD that was left 
can now be placed on the cards 
slots to be used the next Round.
See Cleanup.
8: End of the Round
All the remaining Ready ADs are 
move into the Spent pile. 
All the effect that have the dura­
tion of the Round end now.
See End of the Round.

Initiative
Both players roll a d6 each add­
ing the highest Command value of 
their deployed Units. Units that are 
removed from the table, Pinned or in 
Reserve are ignored for this purpose. 
The winner of this roll gets to pick 
which player has to activate first. 
Note that Characters can increase 
the Command of the Units they are 
in, improving chances of winning the 
initiative.
Reinforcement Waves
The scenario you are playing specifies 
in which Rounds players get rein­
forcements. 
All the cards in the reinforcement 
wave are then placed in front of 
the player and are considered as 
Reserve, available to be deployed 
(see Deploying Units) as one of the 
options during the Alternating Acti­
vation (Phase 5).
Moving Activation Dice
In a standard game each player has 
an AD Pool of 12 and an AD Capacity 
of 8. ADs are used to activate Units, 
React with them, and to perform 
Actions. 
Spent ADs cannot be used for 
anything until they are rolled again. 
When rolled, ADs are moved to the 
Ready pile, and can be assigned 
either during the Pre-assigning 
(phase 4) or during the Alternating 
Activation (phase 5). 
All ADs are either:
•	 On a card’s AD slots: either 
on special Actions for Units, on 
Support cards or on Characters.
•	 In the Ready Pile: available to 
be used to activate Units and 
perform Actions
•	 In the Spent Pile: ready to 
be rolled in future Rounds or 
through Support cards. 
Special rules during the game allow 
to reroll Ready ADs: the player can 
choose one AD in the Ready pile to 
roll again. The new result is kept in 
the Ready pile. Other abilities allow 
to roll Spent ADs: in that case, the 
AD with the newly rolled value is 
moved to the Ready pile afterwards.
At the end of the previous Round the player 
had two ADs left on their cards and one 
Ready AD which is moved into Spent. 
In the new Round they roll up to eight of 
their Spent ADs. If there are too many ADs 
left on cards, the player will roll fewer than 
their allowed number of ADs.

Activation Dice Roll
All players roll a number of ADs up 
to the AD Capacity for the current 
game from the Spent pile. 
Excess ADs remain in the Spent pile. 
Then, players select any number of 
those ADs and re-roll them once. The 
second roll stands as is. The dice are 
then placed in the Ready pile.
Pre-assigning ADs
Players choose any number of the 
rolled ADs and place them on cards.
This allows the player who didn’t 
win the initiative to have some of 
their units ready to use their special 
Actions, and perform better Reac­
tions from the very beginning of the 
game.
Alternating Activation
Players alternate taking turns, 
starting with a player chosen by the 
winner of the Initiative Roll.
Each turn the active player can place 
any number of Ready ADs on card 
slots, then perform one of the follow­
ing: 
•	 Deploy a Unit
(See the Units in Reserve para­
graph) 
•	 Activate a Unit in play
(See the Activating Units para­
graph)
•	 Activate a Support card
(See the Support Cards para­
graph)
•	 Pass the Turn
(See the Passing paragraph)
Placing ADs on Cards
At the beginning of each alternat­
ing turn the active player can place 
Ready Activation Dice on any of their 
cards’ AD slots: an AD placed on a 
special Action slot of a Unit can be 
spent to perform that action when 
that Unit activates and is moved to 
the Spent pile.
The player this Round needed in particular 
to perform a 1+2 ability with a Unit and has 
another 3 Units requiring a 5-6 to use their 
main weapon. 
This result of the roll of 8 ADs isn’t great: 
only two ADs are within 5 and 6. 
The player re-rolls five dice (in red): with the 
new results they can now perform all the 
Actions needed (in yellow and green). 
The remaining dice will still be useful to 
activate the Units.
A player activating a Unit can place ADs on 
the chosen Unit’s slots (A and B) and on any 
other card (C and D).

ADs can also be placed on Support 
cards or Character cards when AD 
slots are present.
Placed ADs cannot be moved again 
during this Round, but placing them 
in advance is useful for Reactions and 
triggered abilities (see the Combat 
section).
Units in Reserve
Units that are not placed on the table 
during the game preparation and that 
are not part of the upcoming rein­
forcement decks are considered as in 
Reserve, and could be deployed as an 
activation. 
A Unit is deployed by placing it in 
any valid Deployment Area allowed 
by the scenario (such as a table edge 
or an area around certain objectives) 
and is immediately activated, but per­
forms one Action less than it would 
in a normal activation, and cannot 
Command. 
Note that no ADs are required to 
deploy a Unit from Reserve.
By default a Unit cannot be deployed 
from Reserve closer than 2 DU from 
an opponent. If there are no areas on 
the table that fulfill this condition the 
Unit cannot be deployed at all. If the 
terrain allows it, the bases of the Unit 
can be deployed in cover. 
Special Actions or Support cards 
allow Units to be deployed outside 
the normal Deployment Areas. In 
particular, disembarking from a trans­
port counts as deployment and the 
bases are placed as close as possible 
to the transport. 
In these cases there are no limits 
regarding the distance from opposing 
Units.
Activating Units
To activate a Unit the player must 
have at least one AD in their Ready 
pile, and move it to the Spent pile.
When a Unit is activated it may 
perform up to two Actions. 
An Action must be performed by all 
the bases of the Unit. The possible 
Actions are:
•	 Unpin: if the Unit is Pinned, 
this Action must be performed 
before anything else 
•	 Movement: it is the only Action 
that could be performed more 
than once per Round.
•	 Control an Objective: necessary 
to score Victory Points in the 
game. See the Playing a Sce­
nario chapter for more informa­
tion about controlling objectives.
•	 A Special Action: Any Action 
listed on a Unit card. Multiple 
special Actions can be per­
formed, but each counts toward 
the two Actions limit. Note 
that Actions marked as FREE 
are Special Actions and count 
towards the limit.
Some special Actions on the Unit 
card present one or more square slots 
with a numeric range in them: these 
are AD slots and to perform those 
Actions the player must have placed 
ADs with a value within the range 
in the slot during Phase 4 (Pre-as­
signing ADs) or at the beginning of a 
Phase 5 (Alternating Activation) Turn 
which are then moved to the Spent 
pile when the action is performed. 
All the other Actions (marked as 
FREE) can be performed without 
spending extra ADs.

Each Special Action can be performed 
only once per Round.
Once a Unit has been activated in the 
Round it cannot be activated again 
or React until the next Round: rotate 
the card to indicate that the Unit has 
used its activation. If any special rule 
(such as a Victory Card) allow for the

Unpin
The action of Unpin removes the 
Pinned status from the Unit (see the 
Pinning paragraph on p. xx).
A Unit that is Pinned must unpin 
before being able to perform any 
other Action, effectively wasting one 
of its actions. 
Movement
Movement is performed in consecu­
tive straight lines of up to 1 DU each. 
The broken line can pass through 
friendly Units, but the movement 
cannot end in a space that overlaps 
the bases of other Units. 
A Unit is allowed to perform multiple 
movement Actions during a single 
activation. 
However, all movement Actions 
after the first are reduced by 1 DU, 
to a minimum of 1 DU.
For example, a Unit with Movement 
3 DU performing 3 Actions can move 
up to 3+2+2 DU.
Facing
Units can change their facing in any 
direction before every 1 DU segment 
of a Movement Action, and will end 
the Action in the direction of the last 
movement segment.
A unit cannot move its full move­
ment in a straight line and rotate 
afterwards, but can spend the last 
segment of movement to perform a 
small movement and  face another 
direction.
Unit Coherence 
If a Unit is composed by more than 
one base, at the end of a movement 
Action all its bases must be within 
1 DU of at least another base of the 
Unit. 
If a base is removed and this con­
dition is no longer true, the Unit 
becomes immedately Pinned. 
When activated, a Unit in such state 
must perform a movement action if 
possible to return within the 1 DU 
limit. 
The last segment of the movement of a Unit 
determines its facing.
Bases must have at least another base 
within 1 DU

Area of Control
Every Unit has an area of control 
1 DU around its bases. Opponents 
passing through it must follow these 
rules:
•	 A Unit cannot enter and exit an 
area of control with the same 
Movement action.
•	 A Movement Action starting 
within an area of control must 
end outside of it or in base con­
tact with an opponent Unit.
The effect of an area of control is 
ignored if terrain elements are block­
ing the Line of Sight or impeding the 
movement betwen the Units, or if the 
Unit is Flying or Jumping.
Controlling Objectives
Controlling and contesting objectives  
is the core mechanics to score point in 
the game. 
As explained in the Scenarios chap­
ter, some Unit types are required to 
perform an action to interact with 
objectives.
Such action should be performed last 
in the activation of a Unit.
Performing Special Actions
As explained in the Special Actions 
paragraph on p. xx, Units can per­
form special actions as part of their 
activation. 
Each special Action can be performed 
only once, and among those are 
virtually all the offensive actions that 
can cause Damage and Reactions 
(see the Combat chapter). 
Unit A starts the movement within the Area 
of Control: it can either move in base contact 
or exit the area, but it cannot freely move 
within the area.
Unit B starts the movement outside of the 
Area of Control: after entering it, it is not al­
lowed to leave it within the same movement 
action. A second Movement Action would 
allow the Unit to exit the Area of Control.
The Socrates has several Special Actions to 
choose from: The Light MG doesn’t require 
extra ADs; the Rocket Pods requires a 1 and 
a 2; the Light Cannon requires an AD with a 
value between 4 and 6, and an AD between 
1 and 2 allows the Unit to jump with one 
Action!

Prepared Actions
Some Special Actions on Unit Cards 
feature a square slot on the right 
side: when the Action is performed, a 
token is placed on the slot to mark it 
as pepared. Units start the game with 
no Action marked as prepared.
Prepared Actions list conditions that 
remove the token, such as triggering 
events or the Pinned status.
Since they are often used as pre-con­
dition for other actions to be per­
formed, often prepared actions are 
described as “Required for” and “lost 
when shooting”.
If a Unit is damaged and the prepared 
Action is disabled, the token is kept in 
place and the effects still apply, but 
the Action of preparing itself cannot 
be performed anymore. 
Character Actions
While activating a Unit you can 
also use any ability of a Character 
assigned to the Unit and, if necessary, 
spending ADs for that. Those abilities 
do not count in the Actions limit of 
the Unit. 
Note that The Character abilities can 
be used only once per Round, even 
if other conditions make the Unit 
activate again.
Commanding Units
Units with a Command value that are 
activated can issue commands to as 
many other Units as their Command 
value, activating them while saving 
ADs: Units do not require ADs to be 
activated through commanding.
After declaring which Units to com­
mand, the Commanding Unit per­
forms all of its Actions, and the other 
Commanded Units are then activated 
in any order.
The commanded Units must have at 
least one base of the commanding 
Unit in Line of Sight and within 2 DU. 
A Unit activated by a command 
cannot use its command value to acti­
vate other Units in that activation. 
Activating Support Cards
To activate a Support card from 
the table a player must spend the 
required number of ADs. 
If no AD slot is present on the card, 
the card can be used without spend­
ing any AD.
After being activated the card cannot 
be used again until the next Round: 
slightly rotate the card to indicate 
that the Support card is consumed.  
A tank (which has Command 2) when 
activating can activate the other two Units 
around it. If the Walker (Command 1) gets 
activated first, however, will only activate 
one other Unit!
A Prepared Action is paid with the required 
ADs (A) and a token is placed in the slot on 
the right to mark it as prepared (B). If the 
conditions trigger it, the effect of the Pre­
pared Action is applied (C).

the card is available to be activated

Cards with the “Single Use” keyword

Passing

All Units and Support Cards are

over into the following rounds. 
End of the Round
At the end of the Round, all the other 
Ready ADs are finally moved into the 
Spent Pile. 
Any rule or ability whose effect lasts 
until the end of the Round ends now.
During the Cleanup and End phases, all

COMBAT
Combat in Full Spectrum Dominance 
is quick but nuanced: players should 
pick their fights wisely, choosing the 
right weapons for the right targets 
and using the terrain to their advan­
tage.
Each weapon can perform one or 
more attacks: each is resolved sepa­
rately. The kinds of armor and weap­
ons are simulated by using different 
types of dice and a threshold-based 
resolution. 
Line of Sight
Shooting is performed from each 
base of the shooting Unit towards 
any base of the target Unit that is in 
Line of Sight. 
A target base is in Line of Sight if a 
line can be traced from any point of 
it to the center of the shooting base 
without intersecting any LoS-block­
ing terrain elements such as buildings 
or rock formations.
In certain conditions enemy Units’ 
bases count as LoS-blocking ele­
ments to determine targeting: enemy 
Infantry bases behind any enemy 
Unit cannot be targeted, Vehicles 
and Mechs are hidden when behind 
other Vehicles, Mechs or Behemoths, 
and Behemoths can only hide behind 
other Behemoths. 
In multi-base Units, a player chooses 
which base will be the shooting base 
performing the Action when deter­
mining Line of Sight. 
Some Actions will be marked with 
a Per Base keyword to indicate that 
each base of the Unit will perform 
the Action, if within range and Line 
of Sight, and it will be treated as a 
Multiple Attack (as described later in 
the chapter). 
The Tank has A and B in Line of Sight, while 
C is hidden. Since they are all enemy bases, 
D is out of LoS for the tank as well.

Shooting Roll
Shooting is performed by rolling a 
number of dice of the type indicated 
by the weapon profile. Each roll that 
is equal to or greater than the target 
Defense is a hit. 
The die result for each hit is import­
ant for resolving the save roll, as 
explained in the next paragraph. 
For example, a light Vehicle shooting 
with its machinegun rolls 3d6 against 
a target with Defense 4. The rolls are 
a 2, a 4 and a 6. The 4 and 6 result in 
Two hits!
Save Roll
For each hit the target Unit performs 
a Save Roll to avoid damage.
The defender rolls the type and num­
ber of dice indicated by the Unit card 
Save stat, and chooses the highest 
among the values. 
If the chosen value is equal to or 
higher than the hit roll, the hit is 
canceled.
Continuing the example from the 
previous paragraph: if the target is 
another Vehicle with Defense d8(2), it 
will roll 2d8 for each successful hit: 
•	 To save against the 4 the rolls 
are 3, 7: the hit is canceled!
•	 To save against the 6 the rolls 
are 4, 5: the hit is not canceled. 
Each non-canceled hit results in one 
damage. 
Units with no Save value cannot can­
cel hits, and the damage is directly 
applied to them.
Pinning
A Unit that receives damage is 
immediately Pinned. 
The first Action a Pinned Unit must 
perform is Unpin, and that removes 
its status. Pinned Units cannot React, 
interact with Objectives, Command, 
and don’t apply the effects of their 
Area of Control. 
A Infantry Unit is Pinned when it 
receives a Hit, even if the hit is 
saved.
The Support Vehicle activates using both the 
MG and the Light Cannon. It rolls 3d6 for the 
former scoring a 2, 3, 6, and 2d6 AP1 for the 
latter scoring a 2 and a 5. 
The target tank receives two hit (the 2s and 
3 miss), and must save with the 2d10 armor 
against the 6. Against the cannon (AP1) it 
rolls only 1d10, becoming more vulnerable.

Armor Piercing
Weapons with the “AP” keyword 
reduce the number of dice in the 
target’s Save rolls by X, 
to a minimum of one die. 
For example, An AP1 weapon 
reduces the amount of save dice by 
one, an AP2 weapon by two, and 
so forth. All weapons used in Close 
Combat (CC) also provide an AP1 
bonus. 
CC and Weapon bonuses do not 
stack.
Multiple Attacks
Some offensive Actions might have a 
x2, x3 or any other multiplier next to 
their name. 
This indicates that when performed 
the attack will be rolled multiple 
times. The target of each attack can 
be different but must be declared 
beforehand, and then all the attacks 
are resolved at once. 
This still counts as a single Action 
and can cause only one Reaction.
Support Attacks from Units
Some Support Cards cause attacks to 
originate from Units. The Unit is not 
activated, doesn’t perform any action 
and cannot trigger Reactions, but all 
the defense bonuses still apply to 
the target of the attack (such as cover 
bonuses or long range bonuses).
Vehicle Facing
For Vehicles, Save rolls against hits 
originating from the rear have their 
dice reduced by one category, to a 
minimum of d6.
A Vehicle’s front is considered to be 
the 180 degrees arc in the direction 
it is facing, across the center of the 
base. Every other direction is consid­
ered rear.
For example, a Unit with Save 1d8(2) 
would roll 1d6(2) against attacks 
from the rear, but a Unit with Save 
1d6(2) would not be affected by the 
facing penalty.
As discussed in the Behemoths 
chapter, larger war machines, follow 
more complex rules, with different 
directions affecting systems with 
their own armor.
Regardless of the shape of the base, a 
Vehicle facing is calculated from the center 
of its base.

Cover
Cover provides additional defense 
to models by increasing the Unit’s 
Defense when being targeted by an 
opponent. 
•	 Vehicles and Mechs get +1 
Defense bonus when in Cover.
•	 Infantry Units get +2 Defense 
bonus when in Cover.
There are various kinds of cover pro­
vided by the terrain (see the Terrain 
chapter) that affect Units in different 
ways. 
In general, Infantry Units can make 
better use of terrain, have higher 
Defense bonuses and are able to 
move across dense terrain where 
Mechs and Vehicles would be stuck.
Cover is ignored if the shooter is 
within 1 DU from the target (see the 
Close Combat Paragraph) or if the 
attacker’s weapon has the Ignore 
Cover (IC) keyword.
Range
As long as the target is in Line of 
Sight all weapons can fire up to twice 
their range value in DU.
If firing from a range greater than 
the weapon’s range characteristic, 
increase the target’s Defense value 
by one.
Some long-range weapons will also 
have a minimum range value: any of 
the opponent’s bases that are closer 
than that range cannot be targeted.
Arc of Fire
Some weapons have a white arc 
symbol to indicate in which directions 
(relative to the model’s facing) they 
can target Units. This is particularly 
common with Behemoth Units.
A Unit touching a LoS-Blocking terrain 
corner (A) or an Obscuring terrain element 
(B) benefit from cover while ignoring it when 
shooting.
Any other Obscuring element between two 
Units (C) provide cover to both sides.
The Unit’s rifles have a range of 2 DU (A).
Targets further than that (B) up to twice the 
range of the weapon (4 DU) gain +1 to their 
Defense value.

Triggering Reactions
An unactivated Unit may spend one 
AD to React when it is declared 
as the target of a ranged attack, or 
when an opponent’s Unit performs 
a movement while in Line of Sight, 
as long as the controlling player has 
at least one Ready AD to use for the 
Reaction.
Even though some Units could poten­
tially trigger several opponent Units 
to React with a single Action, only 
one Reaction can be triggered at a 
time. 
The controller of the triggered Units 
decides whether to React and which 
Unit to React with. However, a Unit 
performing two Actions could trigger 
two Reactions from two different 
Units.
Reacting Units
When a Unit Reacts it can perform 
only one Action of any kind. 
After Reacting the Unit is marked as 
activated and all ADs used for the 
activation and the performed Action 
are moved to the Spent ADs pile. 
Characters’ abilities can be used in 
this phase, and are not counted in the 
one Action limit. 
A Unit that Reacted cannot activate 
or React again in the same Round. 
When Reacting to an opposing Unit’s 
movement, the Reacting player may 
choose the point along the movement 
path to resolve the Reaction.
Combat results between the acting 
and Reacting Units are resolved 
simultaneously. 
Both Units will resolve their Actions 
and results will be applied at the 
same time.
Pinning from Reactions
A Unit could trigger an opponent 
Reaction in its first Action, and as a 
result end up Pinned. 
That Unit must Unpin with the next 
Action available, which could be per­
formed in the same Activation.
For example, a Unit might choose 
to move to get in range, planning 
to shoot in the second Action of the 
Round. If that Unit is Pinned, it won’t 
be able to shoot this Round but it will 
immediately unpin instead.
An individual movement Action 
that triggers a Reaction can still be 
concluded after the shooting rolls, 
regardless of the Pinned status.
When Reacting while shot by A, the tank could decide to shoot back, run away or use other 
special Actions. 
If Reacting to the movement of B, the tank can choose to shoot at it while in the open.
In any case, a Unit that Reacts will miss the chance to activate later in the turn and perform 
multiple Actions.

Multiple Reactions
A Unit performing an Action as a 
Reaction could trigger another Unit’s 
Reaction too. 
For example, in a standoff situation 
multiple Units from both sides could 
choose to React to the first shot fired 
in a chain of Reactions. 
Just like in the case of a single 
Reaction all the combat Actions are 
resolved at the same time, and then 
the effects are applied to all the Units 
involved. 
Note that, as mentioned before, an 
Action can trigger only one Reaction.
Deploying as Reaction
A Unit might choose to deploy other 
Units (either transported, as Reserves 
or through other means) as a Reac­
tion. 
The deployed Units get to activate 
(as part of the deployment, as usual 
performing one Action less), but 
only after all the other Actions of the 
active Unit have been performed.
Close Combat
Any Unit performing an attack 
against a target within 1 DU is 
attacking in Close Combat.
Close Combat attacks gain AP1 
(save rolls are reduced by 1 die) and 
Ignore Cover.
Weapons that have a minimum range 
of 1 DU or greater cannot attack in 
Close Combat, and weapons that 
already have an AP value do not gain 
any further AP bonus from being in 
Close Combat.
Multi-base Units
Infantry Units are usually composed 
of more than one base, and all bases 
must be within 1 DU from each 
other. If this is not the case, the Unit 
must move back in range before per­
forming any Special Action.
Multi-base Units can receive more 
than one lethal hit, with every 
unsaved hit removing one base from 
the Unit. 
When all the bases have been 
removed, the Unit is out of play.
As long as a multi-base Unit has at 
least one base, it can perform all the 
special Actions that are listed on the 
Unit’s card. 
Special actions can originate from 
any base ovf the Unit: the game is 
abstract enough not to require to 
keep track of specific equipment 
within the Unit.
However, any weapon that presents 
the Per Base keyword performs one 
attack originating from each base of 
the Unit instead.
Fighting closer than 1 DU from the oppo­
nent cancels any cover bonus (A) and grants 
a minimum of AP 1 to the weapons (useful 
against B)

If a multi-base Unit receives more

start applying the damage from the 
closest base to the opponent: if a

Contact Weapons
Weapons with the Contact keyword 
can perform an attack only when the 
Unit is in base contact with its target.
For multi-base Units the amount of 
attacks depends on how many bases 
are in contact with an opponent base.
Units attacking in Contact are

Damage Chart
Most Units can survive more than 
one direct hit, but might get damaged 
in battle and progressively become 
more vulnerable and less effective.
If a Unit with a Damage Chart is hit 
and fails to save, roll 1D6 for each 
damage received. 
On the bottom of the card there are 
several colored damage effect boxes 
with numbers indicating the result of 
the 1d6 roll. Multiple numbers could 
refer to the same box: any of the 
indicated results will cause the box 
to be hit.
We recommend using counters to 
indicate which boxes have been 
already hit.
A Unit is destroyed when it receives 
a single damage result on a red box, 
or when it receives a second damage 
result on the same orange box. Mul­
tiple damage results on green boxes 
do not have any effect but might still 
need to be tracked if the effect is 
different than just PIN.

Applying Damage
The effects of the damage rolls are 
listed below, and are applied imme­
diately:
•	 DEAD: the Unit is removed from 
the game
•	 MOV - Movement damage: the 
Unit movement is reduced to 1 
DU .
•	 S1..S4 - System damage: the 
special Action corresponding to 
the line 1 to 4 is not available for 
the Unit anymore. 
•	 ARM - Armor damage: the 
Unit’s Save dice are reduced by 
one category.
•	 PIN - the Unit receives no pen­
alty. Only the normal Pinned 
effect is applied.
If a System is disabled the corre­
sponding special Action becomes 
unusable, and any AD left on the 
Action’s slots is immediately moved 
to the Spent pile. 
Green Damage Slots
A green damage hit any number of 
times without destroying the Unit: 
apply the effect the first time, and 
subsequent hits would Pin the Unit.
If the effect is marked as PIN, it is not 
necessary to track the damage in this 
case, but the Unit is Pinned every 
time is hit on such slot. 
Units Without Damage Chart
If a base of a Unit without a Damage 
Chart suffers damage, it is directly 
removed from the game.
This usually applies to multi-base 
Units, which have specific rules for 
receiving damage.
Damage to Transported Units
Any Unit that is being transported 
by a Unit that is being destroyed is 
immediately deployed and Pinned, it 
cannot perform any further Action, 
and will receive for each base an 
attack with one die of the same 
category as the hit that destroyed the 
transport.
As an example, a Transport receives 
a 3D6 hit, and fails to save two hits 
(a 4 and a 6): both cause a roll on 
the Damage Chart, and both are 
successful kills. The transported Unit 
is placed next to the destroyed Unit, 
marked as activated and Pinned, and 
receives a 1d6 attack for each base it 
is composed of.
Destroyed Vehicles and Mechs
If both players agree, a destroyed 
Vehicle or Mech could be left on the 
table as terrain that provides cover. 
In this case, no more than 2 Infantry 
bases could benefit from the cover 
bonus of a destroyed Vehicle or 
Mech.
In the example above, if a first damage roll 
scores a 4 the Vehicle is slowed. At that 
point, if a second damage roll scores any 
number other than 6 the Unit is removed 
from play. However, if several damage rolls 
will all score 6 the Vehicle will ignore the 
hits and will simply be Pinned every time (if 
not Pinned already).

BUILDING YOUR FORCE
o create their own warband, players 
choose a Faction and build up their 
army list by purchasing a mix of cards 
among Units, Supports and Charac­
ters. 
The sum of the cost of all the cards of 
the warband should be the same for 
all players to guarantee a balanced 
game. 
The Point System
The Point system implemented in 
FSD is fairly simple: its goal is to 
allow players to have balanced fights 
with the warbands they build, but it 
is not immune to min-maxing and it 
does not guarantee that any warband 
will be equally efficient.
For a standard game of FSD, players 
have 60 Points to spend to purchase 
Units, Characters and Supports 
among what is allowed for their 
faction. 
Unless specified in the faction rules, 
a player can build their warband with 
any combination of cards. However, 
since the number of Activation Dice 
rolled every Round is fixed, the 
Actions a player can do in a Round 
are limited and a well balanced 
warband can withstand an unlucky 
AD roll.
Activation Dice
In a 60 Points game players will have 
an AD Pool of 12, and an AD Capac­
ity of 8, limiting to 8 the number of 
ADs rolled each Round.
In case of different sizes of the game, 
the AD Pool size equals 6, plus an 
additional 2 for every 20 Points or 
fraction of it in the warband. The AD 
Capacity is 4 less than the AD Pool.
For example, in a 40 Points game 
players will roll 6 dice per Round 
with a Pool of 10 ADs; on an 80 
Points game players will roll 10 ADs, 
with a Pool of 14.
Unit Cards
The core of the army are the Units. 
Each is represented by one card. 
A Unit card can have one Character 
(represented by a Character card) 
assigned to it. In that case, place 
the Character card behind the Unit 
card, so that all the Action slots are 
showing.

--- Page 44 ---
44
Similarly, a Unit can be transporting 
another Unit, in which case the two 
cards can be stacked to represent 
that.
Character Cards
Characters are assigned to a Unit and 
provide bonuses and special abilities 
to it. Characters do not have their 
own base, and aren’t assigned to 
one specific base of the Unit either: 
they’re considered to be among the 
troops, and their effects are applied 
as long as the Unit is not completely 
wiped out.
Only one Character can be assigned 
to a Unit at any time.
The special abilities that Characters 
have can be used during the acti­
vation of the Unit, and do not count 
in the limit of Actions the Unit can 
perform that Round.
Support Cards
Support cards represent all the 
elements of a force which are not 
present on the battlefield, such as 
external bombardments or recon­
naissance information that provides 
tactical advantages. 
During the Alternating Activation 
phase players can use one Support 
Card instead of activating a Unit.
The support card is then rotated the 
same way a Unit would to mark that 
it is spent for the turn. 
If a Support card has AD slots on it, 
the requirement must be fulfilled for 
it to be activated.
Units and Reinforcements
The cards of a player’s force are orga­
nized in a number of waves that are 
determined by the scenario. Players 
start the game with a first group of 
cards. Units can be set up on the 
table before the first Round begins, 
and the rest are kept as Reserve next 
to the Support Cards.
During the Alternating Activations 
of every Round, players can Deploy 
their Units from Reserve into play.
At the beginning of each Round, 
depending on the scenario, players 
may gain access to the cards of a new 
reinforcement wave: all the Support 
Cards become available to use and 
the Units of the wave are now in 
Reserve (with their Character cards 
assigned to them, if present) and can 
be Deployed.
A Character card is placed right behind the 
Unit card, showing their rules and special 
Actions.

--- Page 45 ---
45
The same cards can be organized in different 
Wave Decks, as long as the maximum point 
value each Round is not exceeded. In the 
examples the points per wave are 28, 37, 
49, 60 (A) and 29, 36, 48, 60 (B).
Each Round the points are within the 
allowed limits.
Constructing Waves
The scenario will specify the 
maximum number of Points that may 
be available (deployed or ready to 
deploy) each Round. 
Before the game, players organize 
their force’s cards into wave decks. 
Wave decks cannot be organized in a 
way that the total allowed Points for 
a Round would be exceeded. 
Wave decks should be placed face up 
in the order they are intended to be 
used. 
The order of Wave Decks cannot be 
changed freely during the game.
Nota Bene: 
Deployment
Units are Deployed during the 
game from Reserve, adding an 
extra layer of strategy to the 
battle, but also losing one action 
on their first activation. 
Before the first Round begins, 
however, Units can be set up on 
the table without being De­
ployed, starting the game fully 
ready and without any action 
penalty.

BEHEMOTHS
Behemoths are an additional element 
of the game, particularly suited for 
veteran players who want to add 
one more level of complexity to the 
battle.
Towering mechanical monsters, giant 
vehicles or self-propelled fortresses, 
Behemoths are the largest war 
machines the factions of Full Spec­
trum Dominance can field. 
A Different Gameplay
Behemoths are not typically used in 
a standard list: they should require 
mutual agreement between the 
players for their use, given that they 
drastically shift game dynamics. 
On one hand, they introduce a nearly 
invincible Unit on the table, while 
on the other a single model uses up 
most of the warband’s points.
However, Behemoths are also an 
excellent way to play asymmetric
games, using nearly the same 
mechanics as the rest of the Units, 
while providing a very different game 
experience.
Behemoths as Units
A Behemoth Unit is composed of a 
single base and follows the same 
rules of Vehicles in terms of objec­
tive control and interaction with the 
terrain. In terms of objectives control, 
the Behemoths count as Mech.
Example of cards to play a large Behemoth. 
Around the Core in the center there are three 
Systems and one Attachment.

--- Page 48 ---
48
Behemoths are represented by multi­
ple cards, which are defined as Core, 
Systems and Attachments. 
Attacks directed towards a Behe­
moth can affect different parts of it, 
depending on the facing: Systems 
might protect the Core of the Unit 
from some directions, receiving some 
damage before letting the hits pass 
through.
Different arc symbols below the 
names of a System indicate which 
direction it shields the Core from.
Core
One card, the Core, represents the 
heart of the Unit, and has all the 
characteristics that you would find on 
a Vehicle card. 
If the Core is destroyed, the Behe­
moth is removed from the game.
The Systems and Attachments are 
connected to the Core, and should 
be positioned next to it, to represent 
the way they are connected. The Core 
lists the Systems and Attachments it 
is connected to. 
Behemoths can offer options regard­
ing both Attachments and/or Sys­
tems: the Tech’s Plato Crawling Plat­
form, for instance, has three different 
weapon options, while the Enlisted’s 
Land Battleship has two options for 
the front System.
Systems
Systems behave similarly to the Core, 
with the exception that they don’t 
have a movement value, nor can they 
be activated separately (except for 
Reactions). Systems have special 
Actions that allow for additional 
firepower or support abilities, usually 
with ranges of fire that are limited by 
the large size of the Behemoth.
Systems are not Units and cannot 
interact with objectives nor perform 
any action not listed on their card.
Attachments
Attachments represent parts of the 
Behemoth that are not large enough 
to be considered individual targets, 
but are still autonomous enough to 
require a separate card, such as large 
weapons.

--- Page 49 ---
49
Activating a Behemoth
Activating a Behemoth requires the 
use of 1 AD, just like any other Unit. 
During the activation the Core 
performs two Actions, and it allows 
for the free activation of any number 
of Systems and Attachments. Each of 
those can perform up to two Actions 
during the activation.
Regardless, unspent Actions of the 
Core or any other System or Attach­
ment cannot be allocated to other 
parts of the Behemoth.
When activating a Behemoth, the 
controlling player can decide not 
to activate some of the Systems, 
and this will allow them to React 
later in the Round. Note that the 
Attachments must always be acti­
vated together with the Core and are 
unable to React.
System Reactions
The Behemoth Core controls all the 
other parts of the Unit and therefore 
cannot perform Reactions.
However, any of the Systems that did 
not activate in the Round can React, 
following the same rules for any 
other Unit: Upon spending one Ready 
AD, the chosen System can perform 
one Action, and is considered spent 
for the Round. 
This means that a Behemoth that 
activated but decided not to use one 
System (but not an Attachment) 
can still React with that System by 
spending one extra AD. 
Similarly, a Behemoth System can be 
used to React earlier in the Round, 
and the Core of the Behemoth can 
still activate later on, activating the 
rest of the Systems and all of the 
Attachments with it.
Movement Limitation
The movement value for the Behe­
moth is listed only in the Core card.
When the Core is activated, the 
Behemoth can perform movement 
Actions. However, it is limited to 
turning to a maximum of 45° before 
any 1 DU segment of movement, as 
opposed to regular Units that can 
rotate in any direction before each 1 
DU segment of their movement.

--- Page 50 ---
50
This limitation reflects the Behe­
moth’s massive size and the difficulty 
of maneuvering such a large war 
machine. 
This means that a Behemoth moving 
2 DU in a single Action will only be 
able to rotate a total of 90° during 
that Action, and even if it performs 
two move Actions in the Round 
(totaling 3 DU), it won’t be able to 
turn completely around to face its 
back.
Receiving Damage
Behemoths are very hard to kill. One 
reason for this is that attacks from 
directions that are protected by Sys­
tems damage the System rather than 
the Core. 
Only hits that pass through the Sys­
tem’s Damage Chart (with a result 
that would destroy a normal Unit) 
affect the Core. When that happens, 
roll directly on the Core Damage 
Chart and apply the effects. 
The System that received the dam­
age is not destroyed, and will keep 
the rest of its functions as long as the 
Core is not destroyed.
When a Behemoth is shot from a 
direction in which no System is pres­
ent (usually the rear), the combat fol­
lows the rules of a normal Unit, and 
the Core’s Defense and Save values 
are used to resolve the combat.
Behemoths do not suffer from the 
effects of attacks from the rear.
The Land Battleship activated its core 
together with the Attachment and some 
Systems, but the Left Plate can still React to 
enemy moving within range.
Don’t forget to spend one AD when a com­
ponent Reacts!

--- Page 51 ---
51
Pinned Cores
A Behemoth Unit can be Pinned only 
when receiving damage to its Core.
Systems cannot be pinned even when 
successfully hit.
Pinned Behemoths cannot React, and 
none of their attachments can React 
as long as the Core is Pinned. 
Behemoth Attachments 
The large weapon attachments of 
Behemoths are capable of few and 
very powerful attacks, that often 
require several preparation steps. 
Depending on the weapon, more than 
one Action could be required for the 
offensive Action to be used. 
Most of the steps to load an Attach­
ment make use of prepared Actions 
(see Prepared Actions on p. xx), 
which are then used as a requirement 
to shoot.
The Attachment has two Actions 
available every Round to set up 
(fewer if the Behemoth is reacting 
that Round), and cannot make use of 
leftover Actions from the Core or any 
System. 
This means that a single shot of the 
Attachment can require more than 
one Round of preparation and a 
considerable investment of Activation 
Dice.
Even though the Corsairs don’t have Behemoths, their larger Rigs behave as such in many 
aspects.

--- Page 52 ---
52
Destroying Attachments
Behemoth Core cards and System 
cards can have the keyword WPN in 
their Damage Chart. A hit landing in 
that slot will destroy the attachment 
marked as WPN.
Attachments usually appear in the 
Core card of the Behemoth, but in 
some cases (such as the Union’s 
Minecrawler) they appear in one of 
the side cards. The attachment is 
however still bound to the Core for its 
activation.
Playing with Behemoths
With a whopping cost of 40 points, a 
large Behemoth may not fit into many 
scenarios. 
The lighter 20-points Behemoths are 
more versatile, and we encourage 
players to field one as a centerpiece 
of experimental lists.
Unlike regular units, Behemoths can 
bend the dynamics of the game in 
different directions depending on the 
scenario, and they we don’t recom­
mend to include them in a list if the 
players are looking for a balanced 
game.
Dedicated Scenarios
In this manual we provide two sce­
narios specifically designed for Behe­
moths, each covering a different type 
of conflict: Clash of Titans, where two 
Behemoth-rich bands of 80 points 
each engage in battle, and The Goli­
ath, which focuses on an asymmetric 
skirmish waged by regular forces 
striving to achieve their objectives 
while evading the powerful attacks of 
their opponents.
These scenarios are set up to give 
Behemoths a more accessible battle­
field with wide unencumbered areas, 
and to create a narrative that fits the 
giant war machines.
Casual Play with Behemoths
We encourage players to experi­
ment with Behemoths with modified 
scenarios to allow a larger number of 
Points (80 or 100): the presence of a 
large Behemoth per side should keep 
the game relatively balanced. 
Make sure to include areas where 
the large bases can move, and some 
objectives that require outmaneu­
vering, to keep the rest of the Units 
relevant!

TERRAIN
The role of terrain elements in Full 
Spectrum Dominance is crucial: not 
only does it provide cover for the 
Units, but it can transform the small 
gaming area into a labyrinth of differ­
ent paths, and, ultimately, choices for 
the players.
Knowing your Terrain
In this chapter, we’ll briefly describe 
the characteristics that each terrain 
element can have, and how those 
affect various types of Units in the 
game.
Before a game, the most important 
thing is that all players are aware of 
the properties of each element on the 
table: some buildings could be pass­
able for Infantry, a low-ceiling shanty 
might or might not provide cover for 
Vehicles, and so forth.
The two main properties of terrain 
are visibility and movement. Apply 
these categories to the terrain ele­
ments of your battlefield with a grain 
of salt, and make sure that everyone 
agrees about them.
Terrain Elements
There are no strict rules on the size of 
each terrain element. Some players 
might prefer to have each build­
ing (and even each piece of scatter 
terrain!) as a separate piece, other 
players instead base their terrain 
on larger bases, each representing 
a whole city block, a hill, or a large 
field.
When referring to terrain elements, 
this means a single distinguishable 
piece of terrain, such as a 5-10 cm 
long wall, or an individual building.

--- Page 54 ---
54
In the case of multiple pieces of ter­
rain glued together, all players must 
agree on how to split them to apply 
the terrain rules.
Visibility Through Terrain
In Full Spectrum Dominance, Line 
of Sight is not measured using the 
actual height of the models and the 
terrain but rather by looking at the 
center of the Units’ bases and the 
shape of the terrain on the table. 
Terrain elements that are tall can 
break the Line of Sight between 
Units, while shorter ones can grant 
some cover or nothing at all.
Terrain can have three kinds of 
visibility:
•	 Open terrain, such as a mesh 
fence or a debris field, does not 
affect the Line of Sight and does 
not provide cover to Units. It is 
either transparent or flat. 
•	 Obscuring terrain, such as a 
corrugated iron fence or a low 
brick wall, does not block the 
LoS entirely but provides cover 
bonuses for most Units that are 
being targeted.
•	 LoS-Blocking terrain is tall and 
opaque enough to completely 
hide Units behind it, blocking 
their Line of Sight. 
If one or more Obscuring terrain 
elements are along the line between 
the center of a shooting Unit and the 
center of its target, the latter is con­
sidered in cover. 
Cover from Terrain
As explained in the Combat chapter, 
Cover has an effect on the Defense 
value of a target:
+1 Defense for Vehicles and Mechs
+2 Defense for Infantry. 
Cover from multiple terrain elements 
is not cumulative and the bonus is 
only applied once regardless of the 
other terrain elements between tar­
get and attacker. 
The Robots in front of the building are ac­
tually considered as in Cover, but the Mech 
isn’t.

--- Page 55 ---
55
Behind the Corner
In addition to the rules of visibility, 
LoS-Blocking terrain can provide 
cover (behaving just like Obscuring 
Terrain) when hiding only part of the 
target’s model base.
In case of multi-base Units the corner 
cover bonus is granted only to the 
bases that satisfy this condition, 
which usually results in only a part of 
the Unit being able to shoot.
Raised Ground
Similarly to corner cover, when a 
model is on top of a raised terrain 
element, it is considered to be in 
cover by opponents at lower levels if 
it is touching the edge of the ground, 
and is otherwise out of Line of Sight. 
This is clearly a simplification and it 
might not reflect reality depending 
on the height of the different ground 
levels and the size of the models. 
Players should agree on what counts 
as raised ground before the game.
Shooting Behind Cover
If a Unit is in contact with an Obscur­
ing terrain, it can ignore it when 
shooting through, but it receives the 
cover bonus when shot at. 
Note that similarly, if a target Unit’s 
model is only partially visible while 
hidden behind a LoS-blocking terrain 
element and in direct contact with 
it, it is considered in cover, but can 
ignore that element when shooting 
through it.
Infantry Cover
In addition to the usual conditions for 
cover, Infantry Units are considered in 
cover in the following cases:
A base of a Unit that is in contact 
with any Traversable terrain is 
considered to be interacting with the 
edge of the terrain element, which 
provides cover from all directions. 
Most of the larger terrain in the game 
that is not a wall or a sealed-off 
building are treated as Traversable. 
If an Infantry Unit is within a Broken 
or Obscuring terrain area, it can use 
its adaptive nature to make use of the 
surrounding obstacles to gain cover 
from all directions.
Movement Across Terrain
Separate from visibility, the move­
ment property describes how differ­
ent terrain features affect how Units 
travel through it in different ways and 
which Units are able to move through 
it at all.
Crossing one terrain element (be it 
a wall section or a building) always 
requires 1 DU of movement, and the 
maximum a Unit can move to cross a 
piece of terrain is 1 DU.
The interior of a huge warehouse can be­
come the location for a firefight.

--- Page 56 ---
56
When a Unit crosses a terrain ele­
ment, its bases are placed on the 
other side of the element.
The following properties describe 
how different Unit types interact with 
terrain. 
•	 Impassable terrain, such as tall 
walls or rocky formations, which 
cannot be crossed by any Unit. 
Only Units that can jump or fly 
can move over the obstacle.
•	 Traversable terrain, such as 
buildings or ruins, can be 
crossed by Infantry. 
Since some Traversable terrain 
elements could be connected 
lines of buildings, it is reason­
able to split them into areas no 
longer than 1 DU. 
Traversable terrain also pro­
vides special cover benefits for 
Infantry (see the Infantry Cover 
paragraph). 
•	 Fragile terrain, such as wooden 
fences or other improvised 
barricades, can be crossed by 
Vehicles and Mechs. 
Furthermore, Fragile terrain can 
also be Traversable, in which 
case it can be crossed by all 
kinds of Units.
•	 Broken terrain is challenging 
for Vehicles (which can cross it) 
but it is ignored by Infantry and 
Mech Units moving through.
Terrain Areas
Terrain elements can represent areas 
where Units can be placed on: it can 
be an Open terrain element such as 
a football field or debris, Obscuring 
like an area of dead ruins or even 
LoS-Blocking like a dense forest 
canopy.
Terrain areas can count as Travers­
able and/or Fragile Terrain, or can 
count as Broken Terrain: Units that 
are affected by such terrain types

action within them have their move­
ment reduced by 1 DU.
Areas on Top of Terrain
Some large terrain elements, such 
as wide buildings with ramps or 
concrete embankments, can allow 
Units to be placed on top of them 
and move as if they were on raised 
ground. 
The properties of the top of such 
terrain elements can be different than 
how they are treated from Units at 
ground level.
For example, a large warehouse with 
a flat rooftop could be LoS-Blocking 
and Passable, while the roof area 
could count as Open and Broken.  
Fighting Through Terrain
It is possible for Infantry Units to find 
themselves on two sides of the same 
piece of Traversable terrain. 
In this situation neither side can eas­
ily cross on the other side, but both 
Units are considered to be fighting 
within the building: both sides can 
attack, but they are both considered 
as in Cover, even if technically closer 
than 1 DU from each other. 
Contact weapons can also be used in 
this particular case, as it’s considered 
that Units are fighting each other in 
contact within the terrain element.
Lastly, weapons with an Area cannot 
be used while fighting through terrain 
elements, since they would hit both 
sides in the process.
Some buildings or fortified positions 
can be used by Infantry as bunkers. 
This could represent concrete fortifi­
cations, notable buildings, or military 
outposts.
When in contact with such terrain 
elements Infantry Units are always 
considered in Cover (even when in 
Close Combat) and gain the Nimble 
ability.
If all players agree, some Strong­

Units inside, in a similar way to trans­
port Vehicles. Entering and exiting 
the building would require 1 DU of 
movement.

Scenario Terrain
In addition to the rules of this chapter, 
terrain elements can have other spe­
cial properties defined in scenarios. 
Some of the most common are:
•	 Terrain elements being 
objectives that can be controlled 
by being in base contact from 
any point of it.
Usually such terrain count as 
Traversable, and therefore 
provide Cover if an Infantry 
Unit is controlling them. If such 
terrain are LoS-blocking Units 
can hide from opponent attacks 
while controlling the objective. 
However, since the objective 
is represented by the whole 
building, opponents can contest 
it by being within 1 DU from any 
point of the building.
•	 Terrain elements that can be 
occupied by Infantry (bunkers, 
tunnel entrances). 
Such terrains can often accom­
modate more bases than their 
relatively small size would 
allow practically, and are usually 
marked as occupied and the 
infantry is temporarly removed 
from the table.
•	 Terrain elements that can be 
destroyed and usually have a 
Save roll and possibly one or 
more hit points. A destroyed 
terrain element could change its 
properties.
•	 Terrain that can be activated 
to change its properties. This 
includes self-destruct com­
mands, gates that can be 
opened and closed, and 
controllable turrets to shoot 
during the game.

Preparing your Table
One of the main reasons to play 
miniature wargames is to field armies 
and terrain on a table in a way that 
feels evocative, and that should be 
the primary concern while preparing 
the battle environment.
However, it’s also important to con­
sider what makes a table balanced 
for the different kinds of forces: 
a lack of large elements makes long 
range guns too lethal, while open 
areas with no cover for the infantry 
makes them far less effective.
Balanced Sides
A simple way to encourage players to 
build tables that are somehow bal­
anced is to task one player to set up 
both sides, and the other player then 
picks which side to play on.
With asymmetric scenarios, the 
player that didn’t set up the table 
decides wether to be the Attacker or 
the Defender.
Rules of Placement
To make sure to give all the kinds 
of factions in the game a fighting 
chance, and for the gaming 
experience to feel the way it is 
intended, we offer some guidelines. 
The rules of thumb to place your 
terrain, which are by no means rigid 
impositions, are as follows:
•	 Light Terrain Density: open Line 
of Sight on most of the area, but 
LoS-blocking elements should 
be placed about 2 DU apart to 
allow Units to move from one to 
the other without ending their 
movement out in the open.
Example: fields and wastelands, 
dotted with sparse buildings 
and occasional fences.
•	 Medium Terrain Density: LoS 
should be almost always broken 
from the two edges of the table, 
and Vehicles shouldn’t be able 
to move 3 DU in a straight line.
Infantry should have enough 
cover opportunities to not have 
to end their movement in open 
space.
Example: Industrial areas, 
alternating between facilities, 
pipelines, and open grounds.
•	 Heavy Terrain Density: Vehicles 
and Mechs should struggle to 
move through such areas, with 
plenty of traversable terrain 
elements for the Infantry. Line 
of Sight is generally limited to 
2-3 DU before a LoS-blocking 
element gets in the way.
Example: Urban terrain, with 
barricades along the roads, 
interrupting the lanes.
Nota Bene: 
Your Table is Unique
Unless a game strictly regulates 
the shape, type, and amount of 
terrain elements a table can have, 
there is no way to make your 
table perfectly balanced.
As long as all players are part 
of the setup of the table and the 
consensus is that nothing has 
been placed in a certain way spe­
cifically gain an edge, this is fine. 
The game experience is never 
gonna be the same on another 
table, or played by a different 
group.

Tiled Battlefields
Some players might not like to use 
a ruler at all, or prefer a hex-and-
counter experience, to play with 3D 
miniatures on a painted table. 
The rules of FSD allow for this option: 
since all the distances are measured 
in DUs, a game can be played on tiles 
rather than using a ruler. 
Assuming that the size of each tile 
equals 1 DU, measuring distances 
and moving Units can be managed by 
simply counting the number of tiles 
traversed. 
Terrain on Tiles
The position and orientation of terrain 
on the tiles is critical for the game: 
even though distances and move­
ment of Units can be managed by 
counting tiles, players have to choose 
where to position each base of their 
Units within the chosen tile.
All rules of cover and contact with 
terrain elements apply just as they 
would for a normal game, and 
Line of Sight is calculated normally 
between Units as well. 
Any movement within the same tile 
(such as getting into cover) would 
count as a 1 DU movement, and 
terrain elements can be placed either 
inside a tile or on the edge between 
two, to act as cover or to slow down 
movement.
It is important that any piece of 
terrain is placed in a way that it 
generates no doubt about which tiles 
it affects: before the game, all players 
should agree on the terrain properties 
of every part of the table.
Size of a Tile
Similarly to what was said in the 
previouis chapters regarding base 
sizes in relation to a value of a DU, 
the size of a tile should be large 
enough to accommodate four stan­
dard bases comfortably. 
Even a tiled battlefield can have a convincing 
look!
A tiled terrain has the advantage of abstrac­
tion, to the point of playing a game of FSD 
as a hex-and-counter traditional wargame!

In certain movements or maneuvres 
more bases might try to fit, and each 
can be considered in the tile if the 
center of the base is within the tile 
limits.
If there is no more space in the tile, 
however, the movement cannot end 
there.
Split Tiles
A linear element (such as a wall) 
placed across a tile splits it into two 
areas. The terrain should be placed in 
an unequivocal way and made clear 
before the game.
Distances on such tiles are calculated 
as normal, but moving across the 
terrain to the other side of the tile 
would follow the rules for the specific 
type of element, usually requiring a 1 
DU segment of the movement to be 
used for that.
Tile Shapes
As long as each area on the table is 
roughly the size of 1 DU, players can 
play on any kind of grid: square tiles 
and hexagonal tiles work equally 
well, providing a reliable system to 
measure distances without the need 
for a ruler. 
The shape of the tiles affects the way 
the terrain is placed and the final look 
of the table as well, and a simple 
crossroad will look different when 
aligned to fit the tiles of a square or a 
hexagonal grid.
Irregular Tiles
Irregular tiles, or node-based terrain, 
should cover all requirements to work 
with this system, but it makes placing 
the terrain in an unequivocal way a 
bit trickier.
Diagonals
In case of a square grid, there are 
tiles that touch diagonally only by 
a corner. To measure distances and 
handle movement in such cases, 
diagonal movements are allowed but 
any second diagonal tile counts as 
2 DU instead of 1 DU. 
This allows Units to cover similar 
distances whether moving diagonally 
or along the tile sides.
Close Combat
Any attack towards an enemy that is 
in the same tile or in an adjacent tile 
is considered as Close Combat.
Attacks in Contact still require the 
models to be effectively in base 
contact: similar to the way terrain 
cover is applied, the position of 
the Units on the table determines 
whether their bases are in contact or 
just very close instead. 
Two Units can be in Contact while in 
different tiles, as long as their bases 
are touching.
If all diagonals count as 1 DU (A) all areas 
end up being squares. With any second 
diagonal counting as 2 DU the distances 
resemble circles more closely.

Multi-base Coherence
Any Unit composed of more than one 
base requires each base to be within 
1 DU of another base of the Unit.
With tiles, this translates to requiring 
every base of the Unit to be within 
the same tile or in an adjacent tile to 
another base of the same Unit. 
Loading and Unloading
When Units unload from a transport 
Unit they are required to be in direct 
contact with the transporting Unit.
However, when playing with tiles, 
the bases of the dismounted Units 
must be placed on the same tile as 
the transport. 
Only if there is no space lein the tile, 
they can be placed on a different tile.

SCENARIOS
Now that the forces are ready, it’s 
time to put them to good use. 
Scenarios help with that, providing a 
context and more importantly a clear 
objective for the battle for all parties 
involved.
Objectives and Victory Points
To win a scenario players must end 
up both with more Victory Points 
(VPs) than the opponent and with a 
positive number of VPs. If all players 
end up with negative VPs there is no 
winner for the scenario. 
The VPs are earned through Victory 
Cards when achieving scenario goals, 
and some VPs can be lost due to 
Units who went out of combat.
Victory Cards are obtained during 
the game - usually rewarded at the 
end of each Round - by completing 
objectives. Each Victory Card can 
be either kept face-down during the 
battle and revealed at the end of the 
game, or instead used for further 
special effects by revealing it, and 
this changes its VPs reward value.
Controlling Objectives
Objectives are tokens or terrain ele­
ments on the table. They can repre­
sent objects standing on the table, or 
instead be elements of terrain that 
blend with the rest of the setup.
To control an objective a player must 
have a Unit controlling it in base 
contact with it at the end of a Round, 
and no enemy Unit contesting it 
within 1 DU from it. 
The act of controlling/contesting is 
verified at the end of the Round, but 
some types of Units need to spend an 
Action to control/contest:
•	 Vehicle Units must spend one 
Action to control/contest an 
objective.
•	 Mech Units must spend one 
Action to control an objective, 
but can contest an objective 
without spending Actions.
•	 Infantry Units don’t need to 
spend any Action to control/
contest objectives.

--- Page 108 ---
108
At the end of each Round objectives 
can be either not controlled, or 
controlled by one player.
Only Units that are not Pinned can 
control/contest an objective. The 
control is not retained if the Unit gets 
Pinned or if in a following Round 
the Unit doesn’t spend the required 
Action.
Retrieving Objectives
Some objectives in scenarios must be 
retrieved. 
To retrieve an objective a control 
Action must be performed without 
the objective being disputed. The 
objective is immediately removed 
from the table and is considered 
retrieved.
Extracting Objectives
An objective that requires to be 
extracted works like an objective 
to retrieve, but it remains attached 
to the Unit that retrieved it and the 
point is scored only when that Unit 
reaches a Deploy Area at the end of 
its activation.
If the Unit is destroyed, the Objective 
is left again on the battlefield where 
the last base of the Unit was killed.
Destroying Objectives 
An objective that can be destroyed 
is described in the scenario with a 
Defense and a save roll and is treated 
as any other target. 
If destroyed, it is removed from the 
table immediately.
Victory Cards
Players gain VPs by drawing 
Victory Cards during the game from 
the 9-cards deck each player has. 
Scenario has different conditions for 
the players to draw Victory Cards, 
but it is generally linked with inter­
acting with objectives, maintaining a 
presence of Units in certain regions of 
the table or destroying certain Units.
Each Victory Card rewards the player 
by a number of VPs which vary 
between 1 and 5. The cards are kept 
secretly in hand, so that the exact 
number of VP a player has at any 
time is hidden from the opponent. 
Most Victory Cards can be revealed 
from your hand at any time to use the 
effect described on the card at the 
cost of the card being worth less VPs, 
and the new value being revealed to 
the opponent.

--- Page 109 ---
109
A player cannot draw more than the 
9 cards from their deck in a game, 
and further draw chances are for­
feited.
Losing Victory Points
In addition to the scenario rules, 
losses among the ranks of a player’s 
warband lead to the loss of VP for a 
player.
Keep a pile of the cards of the 
removed Units to count the losses 
at the end of the battle: for every 5 
Points worth of Units removed from 
play, 1 VP is lost at the end of the 
game. 
Damaged Units or multi-base Units 
with at least one base still in play 
don’t contribute to the VPs penalty.
Reinforcement Waves
As described in the Building Your 
Force chapter, scenarios specify how 
the Points of the warband are divided 
between reinforcement waves.
Having part of a warband joining the 
battle later on changes the balances 
of power drastically, and is one of the 
key elements to considering while 
playing a scenario.
End-game Conditions
The rules to consider a game com­
pleted depends on the scenario. 
All scenarios have a maximum 
duration in Rounds, but other condi­
tions might cause the scenario to end 
earlier.
At the end of every Round, if any of 
the conditions are met, the game is 
over, the Victory Cards are revealed 
and the Victory Points counted to 
check if there’s a winner.
Scenario Modifiers
Some symmetric scenarios can offer 
variants for players that want to 
replay the same game with a twist. 
The variants are generally not as bal­
anced and might add a chaotic ele­
ment to the game, but are an excel­
lent challenge for veteran players.
We recommend to play the scenar­
ios as they are before experimenting 
with modifiers.
Bidding for Modifiers
Optionally, to better balance the 
choice of modifiers, players can 
secretly bid a certain number of ADs 
from their AD Pool to choose which 
modifier to apply. 
The winner of the bid chooses which 
modifier to apply, and for the game 
will remove the corresponding 
amount of ADs from its pool.
The AD Capacity remains unchanged.

Swarm Mode is an expansion for Full 
Spectrum Dominance that introduces 
a solo and cooperative game mode 
against an automated opponent, the 
Swarm.
A new mechanism, the Collective, 
gives players full agency over the 
enemy behaviors without requiring 
complex flowcharts, and offers a 
fresh approach to gameplay. The 
enemies are represented by unique 
Swarm factions that operate through 
a series of Collective cards. 
Playing the Enemy
The human players alternate their 
activations with the Swarm’s, and 
their decisions dictate global Actions 
based on the placement of the 
Activation Dice (ADs) of the Swarm 
throughout the Rounds. 
Players are asked at every step to 
choose between different behaviors 
of their opponent. Balancing the use 
of the Swarm’s ADs is tricky: running 
out of certain AD values too soon 
might lead to bad outcomes, but end­
ing the activations of the Collective 
for a round with spare ADs usually 
translates into further enemies join­
ing the fight.
Managing the Collective becomes a 
game on its own, and a further chal­
lenge that makes the Swarm game­
play unique and different. 
A Variety of Challenges
There are different types of Swarm, 
each with a unique set of Collective 
cards, and all represent the kind of 
forces that move as a whole rather 
than focusing on individual tactics.
Each Swarm features distinct char­
acteristics, Units, and strategies that 
change the game experience while 
fighting against them.
The scenarios in the book can be 
played with every Swarm faction 
and with different levels of difficulty, 
challenging players to come up with 
different tactics for each threat.
Components
Each Swarm faction lists the differ­
ent kinds of Units available, and the 
amount of each. This represents the 
entirety of the Swarm faction forces, 
and throughout the games the total 
number of models in play is never 
greater than that.
All Swarm Units are represented by 
a single base each, and players can 
use the basing convention that better 
appeals to the gaming group and that 
fits the rest of their collection.
To manage the Swarm during a game 
players need a printed copy of the 
faction’s Collective cards, and the 
statistics cards for all their Units.

Example of a Round
Let’s now skim through the steps of a Round, with one player fighting against 
the Hive. We focus on the Hive Collective cards and Activation Dice.
The player picks easy difficulty, and therefore rolls only 6 ADs. They decide to 
re-roll a 2 and a 3. The player then proceeds to activate their first Unit.  
The player chooses one of the two Actions in the first area of the card (placing 
a 3), and places a 2 in the second area. The effects of the two areas can be 
performed in any order, then it is the Player’s turn to activate.
Two more ADs are placed on the second card. Note that if the player had used 
the 1 in the first card, it would have had to necessarily pick the Action marked 
as FREE in the second area of this card. 
The turns alternate until all five cards have been activated. Whenever an area 
has a FREE option, that Action is performed, even if no more ADs are left to be 
assigned.
All the Collective cards must be activated every Round, even if 
the player runs out of activations of their own.
During the Cleanup Phase of the Round, after both the player 
and the Collective have completed their activations, any 
remaining ADs from the Collective are used on the Rally Card, 
and the effects (usually Spawning of new Units) are applied 
immediately.
